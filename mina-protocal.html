Key Insights

  • Traditional blockchains are inefficient in storing their constantly growing data. This problem of state bloat negatively affects the network's decentralization as fewer users are able to participate in chain validation.
  • Mina Protocol is a new-gen layer-1 blockchain that addresses the problem of state bloat. Mina stays a fixed small size of ~11 kB with the power of zk-SNARKs. Beyond decentralization, zk-SNARKs also make Mina more private and efficient than other chains.
  • Other new-gen blockchains have optimized for scalability in the blockchain trilemma, compromising on decentralization in the process; Mina prioritizes decentralization first. Theoretically, any smartphone or browser can run a full node on Mina.
  • However, many of these groundbreaking features for Mina are still under development. The product roadmap is ambitious; the team's ability to deliver will decide the success of the protocol.

According to the blockchain trilemma, improvements in any one of the three desirable goals of a blockchain – scalability, decentralization, and security – come at a cost to the other two. As blockchain technology has improved over the years, the latest generation of blockchains has had some success in overcoming the trilemma. They have focused on scalability, improving throughput by several orders of magnitude but have made various trade-offs to decentralization and security. Solana, for example, has >1,000x transaction throughput of Ethereum but requires its validators to use industrial-grade hardware and commit to consistently upgrading it. This impedes decentralization as the broader community is priced out from taking part in chain validation.

The older generation of blockchains such as Bitcoin and Ethereum continue to suffer from low scalability because they prioritize decentralization. However, decentralization of even these blockchains is under pressure as their size now exceeds hundreds of gigabytes, thus raising the requirements to run a full node. The responsibility is then left to enthusiasts who can afford to spend significant resources towards the good cause of decentralization. In the blog post "The Limits to Blockchain Scalability", Vitalik Buterin warns against significantly increasing a blockchain's parameters to meet the demand for block spaces because it could lead to "extreme centralization" if the average user is unable to run a node.

Mina Protocol

Mina Protocol currently functions as a payment chain and completed the first anniversary of its mainnet launch on March 23, 2022. Smart contracts, called zkApps, are on the product roadmap for Q2 2022. Earlier this month, it raised $92 million from marquee crypto investors such as Three Arrows Capital and FTX Ventures to execute its vision of building a private and secure layer for web3.

While other recent layer-1s have maximized scalability, Mina Protocol has chosen to maximize decentralization. As a succinct blockchain, Mina uses recursive cryptography to keep a verifiable version of the blockchain restricted to a fixed size of about 11kB, versus petabytes for a layer-1 like Solana. Unlike other layer-1 blockchains that grow with every block added, Mina is able to stay a fixed size by using a self-referencing series of cryptographic proofs. It is helpful to think of Mina’s recursive cryptography process as taking a photograph of the blockchain. Whenever a new block is added, another photo is taken of the new block with the existing blockchain, limiting the size of the blockchain to just one picture while keeping all the information from genesis.

With that introduction, this report goes deeper into the problem of state bloat plaguing traditional blockchains and how Mina uses zero-knowledge proofs to address it and, in the process, provides a more private, decentralized, and efficient blockchain. It explores how Mina's design choices and key players enable it to work as a succinct blockchain with support from its native token, MINA. Finally, the report discusses what is yet to come for Mina, its product roadmap, and the team that is building towards achieving its lofty vision.

State Bloat

“The blockchain and state bloat would ever increase the cost of syncing up full nodes until validation is impossible for most users and we degrade to a trusted system. That's why having a conservative hard blocksize cap is so important.”

– Hasu, Strategy at Flashbots

State bloat is the problem of storing the ever-growing data produced by blockchains with growth in their transactions, accounts, tokens, contracts, and other information. To accurately arrive at the current state in a trustless manner, every full blockchain node must store the history of every address used, every token traded, every NFT minted, and any other transactions from the genesis block.

While older blockchains have bloated states due to their long histories, the newer blockchains face these problems due to their high throughput. For example, Ethereum's most popular client, Geth, has a state size of ~600 GB, with about 11 GB added every week. While it can still theoretically run on consumer-grade hardware, as the state size keeps increasing, it may not be possible in the future. Ethereum plans to address the problem of state size in a later set of upgrades called “The Purge". On the other hand, Solana, as a high-performance blockchain, creates data at the rate of 1 GB per second or 4 PB per year. It had initially planned to use a set of "Archivers" to maintain the historical record of transactions while nodes would only store data for the last few days. Later, it scrapped this project with plans to use Arweave’s permaweb to store ledger data. However, Arweave currently only holds 52 TB of data, so it would need to scale significantly to address the demand from Solana. Solana now has no decentralized solution to store transaction history and uses Google’s Big Table as a storage solution in the meantime.

Mina fixes this.

Mina stays a small and fixed size through fancy cryptography called recursive zk-SNARKs, which stands for zero-knowledge succinct non-interactive argument of knowledge.

Recursive zk-SNARKs

Zero-Knowledge (zk)

Zero-knowledge proofs are a way to prove something with the verifier learning no information other than the statement is true. It is easier to understand through an example. Imagine you are on a game show where there is a prize hidden behind one of the thousand doors in front of you. To find the prize, your best strategy would be to open the doors in sequence. If you then wanted to prove to someone that you knew where the prize was, you could tell them the door number, and they could verify it themselves. This would be simple proof that you have the answer, but it crucially shares the solution with the verifier. However, if you blindfolded them and spun them around before taking them to the door with the prize, you could still prove that you knew where the prize was without sharing the door number. This would be a zero-knowledge proof. It confirms that you, the prover, know the solution without leaking it to the verifier. It’s worth noting that there is also an asymmetry of work. The amount of work done by the prover to find the answer is much more than that done by the verifier to check if it is correct. The prover has to search through each door till they find the correct one, whereas the verifier only needs to check one door.

Succinct Non-Interactive Arguments of Knowledge (SNARK)

SNARKs are a type of zero-knowledge proof. They are called succinct because they are small and easy to verify. A SNARK proof is ~7 kB on Mina and verifying it only requires 200ms.

While some zk proofs can require a back-and-forth exchange of information between the prover and verifier, non-interactive proofs can be authenticated by the verifier without more interaction with the prover.

Arguments are a formalism for proofs. In cryptography, proofs can only be generated of valid statements, while invalid argument proofs may be generated by using exorbitant computational power. However, that is only a theoretical difference, and for our purposes, we can consider arguments to be the same as proofs.

Knowledge refers to the fact that the prover has the answer. In our example, they do not just prove there is a prize, but they also that they know which door it's behind.

Therefore, SNARKs are small, easy-to-verify proofs of knowledge that do not need back-and-forth communication between the prover and verifier.

Recursive

Finally, Mina uses a type of zk-SNARK called Pickles that can recursively refer to itself, thus creating proofs of proofs and keeping the blockchain a fixed size. An additional property of Pickles is that, unlike other SNARKs, it does not require a trusted setup. A trusted setup is considered less desirable because future users must trust that the original setup was performed fairly with adequate controls.

Mina and zk-SNARKs

With the help of zk-SNARKs, Mina can have a version of the blockchain that remains a small and fixed size. Whenever a new block is added, a new zk-SNARK carrying the previous block’s zk-SNARK must be created to prove it is valid. This SNARK can only be created if the block’s data is correct. Therefore, a SNARK proves that the current block is valid and references the previous block’s SNARK that proved that it was valid. In this way, the entire state of blockchain can be confirmed just by verifying the current SNARK because it must have been generated on top of a series of valid SNARKs from genesis.

However, a cryptographic proof is not enough to run a full node. The proof alone does not allow the node to perform its essential functions because it does not supply clear information, such as the account balance. In addition, a node needs four more pieces of information to function. The first is the protocol state containing hashed data structures, including the ledger. The second is a verification key for the SNARK proof and the protocol state. Next, a node must also store account information and a Merkle path that matches the protocol's to trustlessly ensure the account information is correct and pertains to the current protocol state.

While the Mina marketing material claims the blockchain is ~22 kB, with improvements in technology, it will just be ~11 kB.

11 kB, really?

Well, not entirely and not right now. Since such a node does not have the complete history of transactions, it cannot participate in consensus and is thus called a non-consensus node. However, it is much more powerful than a light node of a traditional blockchain as it operates without trust assumptions. It can independently verify the blockchain data, pull its account balance, and broadcast transactions. In this respect, it is similar to a full non-mining node on Bitcoin or Ethereum. Another thing to note is that non-consensus nodes are currently not live; they are being developed by the teams support Mina Protocol.

Benefits of zk-SNARKs

Mina’s implementation of zk-SNARKs makes it a compelling alternative with unique benefits over traditional blockchains.

Improvements in decentralization

Running a non-consensus node requires only a tiny amount of disk space and computing power. While other blockchains suffer from state bloat and may require powerful industrial-grade hardware to run full nodes, Mina's non-consensus nodes will run on smartphones or browsers. Every user would be able to run their own node, significantly improving decentralization. The blockchain ideal beyond self-custody is self-verification, and Mina is the only blockchain that will enable it.

Asymmetry of work

For traditional blockchains, every node must execute every transaction independently, leading to wasted computing resources and higher transaction costs. Verifying a transaction's zero-knowledge proof is much less resource-intensive than executing the transaction itself. This is the premise of zk-rollups on Ethereum, which Buterin considers the most crucial piece in the journey to scale Ethereum. Mina, however, has zero-knowledge proofs baked into its protocol design.

Privacy

Zero-knowledge proofs support privacy by not leaking any unnecessary information. A zk-proof of the Mina blockchain only proves that the state is valid without showing the interacting accounts. Even Mina’s consensus nodes only keep a history of the last 290 blocks.

Consensus

Mina uses a consensus mechanism called Ouroboros Samasika, a modified version of Cardano's consensus mechanism, Ouroboros. Ouroboros Samasika has the added property of working with a succinct blockchain that does not keep an entire history of transactions. Like other proof-of-stake chains, the probability of being selected as a block producer depends on the amount of Mina staked by a node relative to the total stake. Like Cardano, Mina does not require nodes to lock funds, nor does the network slash funds. The network stops distributing rewards to nodes that go offline or engage in bad behavior.

The network does not know nor announce the next block producer, and multiple block producers may qualify. This ambiguity adds to the protocol's security by creating a natural defense against denial-of-service attacks against block producers. However, it has the disadvantage of sometimes creating short-term forks. If multiple block producers produce different valid blocks, the next block producer follows standard consensus rules and builds on top of the longest chain. If only same-length chains are available, block producers build on top of the first-seen chain or replace it if another one has a higher verifiable random function output.

This design choice also means that Mina has probabilistic finality. With a 90% honest stake and 4-minute block time, Mina reaches 99.9% finality in 15 blocks, i.e., 60 mins. This is much longer than some other new blockchains like Solana and Avalanche that take seconds to finalize transactions.

Transaction Process

There are two important players in the transaction execution process for Mina: block producers and SNARKers. Block producers are similar to validators of other proof-of-stake chains. They are randomly selected to produce a block based on the percentage of their stake compared to the total stake. SNARKers or SNARK workers produce the SNARK proofs of individual transactions of the blockchain. To better understand their roles, let's look at the transaction life cycle on Mina.

  1. First, to execute a transaction, users broadcast it to the network with the information of the fee they are willing to pay, which gets collected in the transaction mempool.
  2. Independently, SNARKers continuously work on supplying SNARK proofs of these transactions so that they can be included in the block.
  3. As a block producer is selected, they queue the most profitable transactions with the highest fee from the transactions available in the mempool. Block producers, however, must also add SNARK proofs of an equal number of transactions as they are adding to the queue. They can either produce the SNARK proofs themselves or buy the SNARK proofs from the SNARKers.
  4. SNARKers compete amongst themselves to supply the SNARK proof at the lowest cost. As per Mina Explorer, over the last 100 blocks, all SNARK proofs were provided for free by the SNARKers. SNARKers may later be subsidized by protocol incentives similar to mining, called SNARK mining.
  5. The block producer then updates the queue, which keeps a constant size as the number of new unSNARKed transactions equals the number of transactions deleted after being SNARKed.
  6. The block producer then includes the SNARKed transactions in the block and updates the zk-SNARK proof of the protocol state.
  7. The new block and SNARK proof are gossiped across the network and confirmed by the other nodes.

Source: Mina Protocol Whitepaper

Censorship Resistance

Mina uses a fee market and is similar to Bitcoin in that each transaction waits in the mempool till block producers add it. Usually, a user can ensure that block producers add their transactions to the blockchain by paying a high-enough fee or waiting long enough.

For a transaction to be censored, either all SNARKers would have to refuse to produce the SNARK proof, or all block producers would have to refuse to add the SNARKed transaction to a block. Under Ouroboros, we can presume that the network is decentralized, and the block producers are not colluding.

Collusion amongst all the SNARKers is extremely hard. First, there is no barrier of entry for anyone to SNARK a transaction. Because the cost of SNARKing is tiny, anyone can produce a SNARK proof of a transaction and earn the SNARKing fee. Even with a low transaction fee, a block producer should profitably be able to include a SNARKed transaction in a block.

Network Stats

Mina is a relatively new blockchain with unique architecture. As such, only a limited amount of data is available with respect to the network and its adoption. We have compiled the following information from the currently available explorers and dashboards.

Mina's theoretical throughput is one transaction per second. Clearly, Mina does not compete with other newer blockchains on throughput volume. Instead of just comparing blockchains on their throughput numbers, Mina tries to compete on a more holistic metric of scale per unit of decentralization or ScaDe. The ScaDe frontier is a corollary of the blockchain trilemma that suggests that the amount of throughput of a blockchain is inversely proportional to the number of full nodes. Because Mina aims to maximize decentralization with no theoretical limit on the number of nodes, it operates outside the ScaDe frontier.

The MINA Token

As the blockchain’s native token, MINA is used to pay transaction fees and incentivizes consensus participation through block rewards. Currently, the governance process does not have token-based voting, whether on-chain or on the forum, although there are plans for it.

MINA is an inflationary currency with initial inflation of 12% that will reduce to settle at 7% after four years from mainnet launch. Inflation is fixed, and staking yields change with the staking participation rate. While inflation may seem high, it helps to keep the chain secure as it encourages staking participation.

There are multiple ways to look at the supply of MINA, such as a circulating supply that excludes time-locked tokens or a fully diluted supply that includes them. The simplest and most accurate way is through staking supply. MINA had an initial total supply of one billion tokens, of which 806 million were available for staking at launch. Most of these tokens were locked for selling but were available for staking, thus earned block rewards. The remaining 194 million tokens of the initial supply are being distributed to the community over time in the form of supercharged rewards, SNARK mining rewards, and ecosystem grants.

Supercharged Rewards

Recently, there was a debacle with Looks Rare and its staking rewards. Similar to Mina, insiders held locked tokens that were not a part of the circulating supply but could be staked to earn rewards. This allowed them to earn a significant proportion of staking rewards in the initial days after launch as their holdings were much higher than the circulating supply. Notable Twitter personality, Cobie, covered this in his recent blog titled, "Incentives Structures”.

Mina avoided this controversy before it took place by having a better-designed rewards structure. In the first 15 months after launch, users who stake unlocked tokens receive increased block rewards called supercharged rewards. Currently, the rewards earned by unlocked stakers double those of locked stakers, allowing for a more community-favored reward distribution and avoiding the controversy faced by the Looks Rare team.

Roadmap

Mina has an exciting product, but the team is still building its most groundbreaking features. It currently only functions as a payment chain with a few hundred validators. Smart contract functionality, non-consensus nodes, and an oracle that can trustlessly pull data from the internet are promising features slated to be added in 2022. Mina also has plans to develop a zk-Rollup that stores data off-chain, which will enable it to scale its throughput.
