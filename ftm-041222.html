Key Insights

  • Fantom launched during the ICO boom of 2018. The protocol was quiet for a few years as it built out its technology stack, before picking up momentum throughout 2021 and into 2022 as it focused on DeFi.
  • The project’s technology could fundamentally improve existing methods. Fantom attempts to solve the blockchain trilemma by utilizing a directed acyclic graph (DAG) structure for its nodes which enables higher throughput for the protocol.
  • While TVL grew impressively in 2021 and early 2022, the departure of key contributor Andre Cronje coincided with a sharp drop in TVL and a daily new address. Developer activity and transaction volume have also declined.
  • Fundamental metrics like revenue, price to sales, and TVL to market cap remain strong relative to peers, but it remains to be seen if these metrics were driven in large part by Andre’s influence.
  • Even before Andre left, the spiking transaction costs during periods of high activity were a potential red flag to scaling the network.
  • Overall, the protocol has some great things going for it, but the ghost of Andre looms large, making it a speculative investment at this time.

What makes a compelling Layer-1 protocol?

Strong technology? Table stakes. Team with solid backgrounds? Of course. Sound tokenomics? Yes, please. Strong ecosystem? Have to have it. Meeting an unsolved problem? Why else would we be here?

All major Layer-1 projects today check these boxes. What sets a Layer-1 apart is actual adoption and usage.

One protocol meets all of these criteria, and yet, it doesn’t seem to be on anyone’s radar. This protocol is the sixth-ranked protocol in terms of total value locked (TVL) and is the only major protocol with a market cap to total value locked ratio value under one.

So why are people scared to talk about Fantom?

Protocol and Team Overview

Fantom was created in 2018 by Ahn Byung Ik, a Ph.D. in computer science and founder with multiple prior exits. Little is mentioned about these early days on Fantom’s official channels, but there are indications the project did not have the robust technology it initially pitched. Ik and the early team left the project entirely in 2019. Fantom was quickly picked up by the well-regarded Andre Cronje and current CEO Michael Kong. Cronje is a serial DeFi founder with a strong reputation in the space. He built and helped drive popular protocols Yearn.Finance, Keep3r Network, and Multichain.xyz to billions of dollars in value locked, almost overnight. Cronje helped spearhead the technical aspects of Fantom in its early days and helped launch many popular Fantom apps before apparently departing crypto entirely in March 2022. Anton Nell, Andre’s partner, and another major technical contributor, also decided to step aside. Today, Fantom is led by Kong at the helm of the Fantom Foundation.

The early goal of the protocol was to be the backbone of smart cities but has since pivoted to focus more on DeFi. Today, Fantom boasts one of the most used DeFi platforms among all Layer-1s.

Technology

Fantom is a Proof-of-Stake Layer-1 smart contract platform competing directly with Ethereum, Solana, Avalanche, and others. Fantom calls their ecosystem Opera. The core architecture of the Fantom Opera ecosystem is made up of two main layers: the Ware layer and the Consensus (core) layer.

Consensus Layer

Fantom attempts to solve the blockchain trilemma by utilizing a directed acyclic graph (DAG) structure for its nodes. DAGs are well-known database structures for transmitting data throughout a network. They are characterized by a web of nodes rather than a chain. This structure increases throughput because transactions don’t need to be serialized like they do in a traditional blockchain.

Source: Zipmex

Each Fantom node stores a local DAG composed of events blocks containing lists of transactions. As transactions are confirmed on an individual node, they are periodically shared with other nodes. Once transactions are confirmed by enough independent nodes, blocks are batched to form the finalized blockchain This structure allows Fantom nodes to process transactions faster and more efficiently than data structures on other chains. For data availability, DAGs are separated into sub-DAGs called epochs which are composed of finalized batches of transactions.

Source: Fantom Whitepaper 2.0

The DAG structure is the underlying technology of a novel consensus mechanism called Lachesis. Lachesis is based on the principles of asynchronous Byzantine fault technology (aBFT). Traditional BFT networks require nodes to constantly communicate as they send each other blocks for validation. This type of communication becomes an issue as the network scales. Lachesis does not suffer from the same constraint.

Lachesis is asynchronous, meaning nodes can independently reach consensus. They then periodically exchange transactions to agree on what has recently happened on the network. There is no mempool. Nodes don’t have to wait for other nodes to reach consensus. Transactions can be confirmed as soon as they enter the network. To explain Lachesis, Fantom’s CEO King likes to use a train analogy. For Bitcoin, trains come every ten minutes, and you have to hope there is space for you when that train comes along. With Fantom, trains are always waiting and never full, and you can access them on demand.

In a DAG, all users are both issuers (i.e., they make/send transactions) and validators. This structure allows consensus to also be leaderless: there is no one validator who has to propose a block. The entire network doesn’t have to agree on one specific block at one time. There is a network of “primary” nodes confirming transactions while secondary or monitoring nodes, serve as a second checkpoint. Misbehaving actors forfeit 100% of their staked FTM. This is a huge disincentive as the minimum stake is currently over a half-million dollars.

The consensus is deterministic versus probabilistic. In Bitcoin or other blockchains, the longest chain determines what is canonical. After a certain series of blocks, usually 10–60 minutes worth depending on the blockchain, we can determine that it is very unlikely that our transaction will be reverted. The transaction is considered final. Due to the enhancements we discussed, Lachesis allows for 1–2 second finality.

Overall, this novel technology stack has proven extremely reliable, avoiding any downtime and network issues even during times of extreme volume.

Ware Layer

Fantom’s Ware layer provides open-source APIs and smart contract functionality. The main codebase is written in Solidity, and applications are EVM-compatible. Fantom is developing its own Fantom Virtual Machine (FVM) to replace the EVM in the Ware layer. The FVM timeline has yet to be determined. Payments on the Ware layer for smart contract usage are made in the FTM token.

Leveraging the EVM, the Fantom mainnet was officially launched in late 2019. The ecosystem did not have much functionality in its early years, and as the technology grew the network finally hit an inflection point in 2021. Today, the Ware layer has flourished into a network with extensive smart contact activity. The majority of the activity is focused on DeFi. Apps focusing on gaming and NFTs are slowly springing up, but the majority remain in their infancy. Below is a visual of some of the key apps on the ecosystem. As you can see there are many of the Ethereum native primitives users know and love like Metamask, Curve, and Dai.

Key Ecosystem Applications

Source: DeFi Llama

SpookySwap

SpookySwap is the most popular automated market maker (AMM) operating on Fantom. The platform is essentially Fantom’s version of Uniswap. Spooky has ~14% of TVL on Fantom and is the largest DeFi protocol on the network. Across all protocols, it is usually a top-three exchange in terms of volume. Users can swap tokens and add liquidity to pools to earn a share of trading fees. Users can also stake tokens to earn the native token BOO. SpookySwap improves on other AMMs with limit order functionality and native bridging capabilities. Spookyswap is not only popular but also extremely profitable with $133 million in annualized revenue and trading at a 1.4x price-to-sales ratio, one of the lowest of any protocol tracked by Token Terminal.

Geist Finance

Geist Finance is the most popular native lending platform on Fantom, similar to AAVE on other platforms. It contains roughly $500 million in TVL. Geist takes a unique approach to the banking model in rewarding borrowers. Lenders and borrowers receive 50% of protocol fees subject to a 3-month lockup (2:1 in favor of borrowers). The remaining 50% of protocol fees are distributed to Geist stakers. In the first year, 65% of all GEIST tokens in existence will be distributed as rewards. Geist’s token price has dropped from $3.70 at launch down to $0.11 at the time of writing mainly due to the inflationary nature of this reward structure. Another unique aspect of Geist is that it is completely decentralized: no governance and no venture capitalists. Scream is another similar platform on Fantom that is a direct competitor to Geist.

Source: Geist Finance

Solidly

Solidly is a new AMM, also based on Uniswap, introduced by Andre Cronje. The protocol initially received massive adoption in the Fantom ecosystem, bringing in $2 billion in TVL in less than a week before losing 80% of its TVL after Andre departed the project. The original goal was to improve upon the popular vote escrow (ve) token model pioneered by Curve. Curve is an AMM primarily focused on stablecoins. For providing liquidity to pools, users receive Curve tokens as well as a proportion of trading fees charged to users. These users are then incentivized to lock their Curve governance token for further rewards. They are awarded veCRV based on the length of time the Curve government tokens are locked up, thereby giving them more governance power over the protocol. This is great for users in that they get more rewards, great for the protocol because it encourages users to lock up funds there, but not great for users' liquidity as rewards are locked.

On Solidly, the locking mechanism is similar to Curve in that users can lock tokens for 1, 2, 3, or 4 years and receive a commensurate reward the longer they lock their tokens, but the rewards they receive are distributed in an NFT which is freely tradable. Solidly also makes some other novel improvements on the Curve model to further incentivize ecosystem participants, the most notable one centered around token voting rights. Solely focuses on incentivizing what the protocol ultimately cares about (fee income) rather than pool size.

Investors

Fantom raised $40 million across two funding rounds in 2018. Throughout 2020 and 2021, follow-on investments came from other major investors including Alameda Research, Hyper Chain Capital, and BlockTower Capital, totaling over $60 million. Over 20 smaller venture capital funds are also investors. Investors have steadily come back into the fold as the technology stack was built and the product-market fit was more borne out across 2021.

Partnerships

Fantom has partnered with numerous nation-states to use its technology. Tajikistan to power government information, Ukraine for intellectual property exchanges, Pakistan for education, and Uzbekistan on a CBDC and supply-chain use cases. This use case for their technology is fairly novel among Layer-1s as nation-state adoption is generally lacking. Many of these partnerships are still in their infancy but show the reach of the Fantom ecosystem.

Tokenomics

Usage

Fantom uses FTM as its native token. FTM’s uses include as a governance token, network security mechanism via staking, and payment mechanism for transactions and fees. For network security, stakers are required to lock up 500,000 FTM and maintain industrial-grade hardware (AWS EC2 m5.xlarge with 4 vCPUs (3.1 GHz) and at least 1 TB of Amazon storage). Validator size is capped at 15x the self-stake amount. Stakers who meet these requirements and decide to run their own node can receive yields above 15%.

Users with less FTM can delegate their stake. Stakers who delegate can lock up their token for up to a year to earn 12–13% or stake as they go for roughly 5%. Inflation averages roughly 7% per annum. Stakers earn 70% of all transaction fees paid by network users, with the remaining 30% being held in a special fee contract (SFC) controlled by governance. Fantom allows for reinvestment of staked assets keeping these assets fairly liquid. Staked FTM can be redeemed for sFTM or aFTMb which then can be used for yields farming, trading, lending, etc.

As a governance token, one FTM equals one vote, with each governance proposal submission costing 100 FTM. FTM has the unique mechanism of ranked-choice voting for proposals which enables greater flexibility as compared to binary votes. A minimum level of agreement across voters is required for network changes, ranging from 55% for minor updates to 90% for major updates.

Source: Fantom Foundation

Overall the token accrues value through staking rewards and network fees. The more users transact and interact with ecosystem applications, the more value the token should accrue in the form of cash flow. The supply of the token is fixed, similar to a network like Bitcoin, allowing more certainty when considering future value.

Source: Fantom Foundation

Distribution

The entire supply of 3.175 billion tokens was minted or “pre-mined” at launch. 37% went to private investors and ~3% to public investors. 31% of the distribution was allocated to paying future staking fees. 25% was allocated to project advisors and the founding team. The final 4% was allocated to a strategic reserve. The allocation to private investors, advisors, and insiders at 62% is higher than OG Layer-1s like Ethereum, Bitcoin, and Cardano (all under 25%), but in line with newer Layer-1s like Solana, Avalanche, BSB, NEAR, ICP, and Polkadot (all above 50%).

Source: Messari

Supply Schedule

The token unlock period was heavily front-loaded with most unlocks completed by early 2020. The remaining distributions of the pre-mined tokens will be for inflation rewards. About 600,000 FTM are awarded to stakers per day until this supply runs out in 2024. At this point, the network must be mature enough for stakers to be rewarded through transaction fees alone or the reward structure must be adjusted — more on this in the section on risks.

Source: Messari

Developers

Fantom had strong developer growth in 2021. Fantom quadrupled the number of total developers it had compared to 2020. It tripled the number of full-time developers. On a percentage basis, the number of total new developers was the highest of any major protocol.

Source: Electric Capital

In August 2021, a 370 million FTM developer incentive fund was announced to help to continue to drive developer growth. The program is fully programmatic, with no arbitrary approval process. Protocols are rewarded based on their time-weighted average TVL. Unfortunately, this has allowed the incentive rewards so far to be gamed. Still, only ~20 million of that FTM was distributed in 2021, leaving 350 million FTM or (over $500 million) for developer rewards in 2022 and beyond. We’ve seen these types of funds drive strong ecosystem growth for other major Layer-1s like NEAR ($800 million fund) and Avalanche (multiple $200+ million funds).

Competition

Fantom’s DAG-based model allows the protocol to be much faster and cheaper than most of the competition. They also have a time to finality that's one of the best in the business. Still, these advantages come with the trade-off of centralization. Running a Fantom node costs over $500,000 and requires some serious hardware, and as a result, there are fewer than 100 nodes currently operating. We’ll look to see if Fantom can maintain the technical advantages as they scale while increasing the number of validators on the network. Right now most of their metrics compare favorably to the much more popular Solana and Avalanche.

Future Roadmap

Standalone EVM: Fantom is doing research to build an eventual replacement to EVM within their ecosystem. The EVM is a stack-based VM that has to process transactions in order rather than in parallel. This creates numerous bottlenecks as well as storage issues. Fantom is building a new register-based virtual machine to improve throughput at the base layer. The end product will be an entirely new virtual machine, new compiler, and new interpreter that will enable higher transaction speed with lower data storage requirements. These improvements will allow faster smart contract execution than the EVM allows. The team claims that in testing the FVM is able to process 10x more transactions when compared to the EVM.

Storage Updates: The size of the existing DAG is growing very quickly and will soon approach a size that could lead to onerous hardware requirements for smaller validators as they come online. Fantom plans to explore multiple storage solutions to meet this challenge. One solution is decentralized solutions to store the history of the blockchain by Arweave and others. Fantom is also exploring flat storage data structures to allow nodes to more quickly access data.

More On-Ramps: Fantom is looking to add more fiat to FTM gateways over 2022 to allow for easier onboarding of users. Onboarding users remains a common pain point for ecosystems, particularly protocols not listed on major exchanges like Coinbase as is the case with Fantom.

Scalability Upgrades: Over the next few months network upgrade Snapsync will be rolled out. Snapsync will allow the network to download blocks quicker, allowing network participants to download the full state of the network faster. The upgrade will reduce the overall state (2.7 TB to 270 GB). Further enhancements for storage and node structure will be coming as well, particularly surrounding the RPC nodes which will reduce memory consumption by 90% (from 90 mb/s to 7 mb/s).

Valuation

Bear Case

New addresses holding a token are generally tightly correlated to token price. The intuition is simple, the more new users that are interested in a protocol during a particular time period, the more value the native token accrues as those users onboard and utilize the ecosystem. This reflects the new incremental demand above the steady state of the current user base.

Historically, there has been a strong correlation of roughly 0.7 between FTM token price and new addresses obtaining FTM; a strong correlation. As seen in the chart below, new addresses have steadily ticked down since January. This fall began even prior to the departure of Cronje. New addresses stagnated and grew slightly with the launch of Solidly. This slide continued in March despite FTM price rebounding along with the broader crypto market. If the prior strong correlation between new accounts and token price continues to hold, Fantom should be trading closer to $1.

Fantom has a fairly high transaction volume — it is by no means a ghost chain. But while daily transactions volume is mostly in line with major Layer-1s, it has come down from the start of March. Daily transactions peaked in September 2021 and have stayed in a constant range for the past six months despite broader network growth. The spike in February 2022 can be attributed to the launch of Solidly. Network usage has stagnated since October while other chains have seen their transaction volume trend upwards over that time period: NEAR has seen 2-3x volume and Avalanche 3x-4x.

While developer additions were strong in 2021 as we discussed earlier, actual developer activity has been weak in 2022. Looking at the repositories and branches of a protocol to measure commit activity, Fantom ranks 35th among protocols. New commits to Fantom have notably declined since Andre left at the start of March.

To contribute to the headwinds, the protocol has an usual monetary supply schedule. Over 400 million FTM will come into the ecosystem in the next year and a half or so in the form of staking rewards. This distribution of new supply should further dilute the overall value of the existing tokens. Inflation then completely subsides in 2024 as the pre-mined reward pool is extinguished, presenting a new issue for the protocol as to how to adequately reward stakers.

Bull Case

The bull case for FTM is driven by fundamentals. Looking at Fantom versus other Layer-1s paints the protocol in a very positive light. Fantom has more revenue than Solana at less than 1/10 the market cap. It also has a very attractive price-to-sales ratio, even compared to the reigning Ethereum.

FTM is undervalued when looking at the TVL relative to market cap compared to the competition. They also generate more revenue per dollar of TVL. These are important metrics to gauge network usage and conflicts with some of the new address data we discussed in our bear case.

FTM also could have further demand-side pressure incoming from retail users. FTM is a very retail-friendly chain with its marketing (ghost-themed), low token price, relatively low VC count, and focus on fair launches. It is not listed on major US exchange Coinbase which could provide a nice tailwind if and when it is added. Fantom also has plans to launch its own native cryptocurrency exchange powered by Binance Cloud. This exchange will allow for deeper liquidity of native Fantom ecosystem assets.

Risks

Gas Price Spikes

Although Fantom claims to be very cheap, that isn't always the case, at least at the current juncture. Transaction spikes in January and February led to surges in gas prices. These were periods where the DeFi primitives Fantom is known for were being used the most. Gas fees peaked above $1, which is much lower than Ethereum but much higher than the average price of <$0.01 the network usually has. The hope is that the SnapSync upgrade mentioned earlier will alleviate some network congestion and lower gas.

Rough Beginnings

Indications from the current team are that the original project was essentially vaporware. CEO Michael Kong notes that the original whitepaper for Fantom was likely plagiarized. The original team left in a hurry after the technology was discovered to not be what the broader Fantom Foundation thought it was. Fantom missed out on a lot of ecosystem growth in 2019, and 2020 as they rearchitected their technology stack. The rough start for the project left a bad taste in many investors’ mouths. To be fair, the current FTM has been exceptionally forthright about this, discussing it at length in multiple interviews. Still there remains the possibility, however small, that the original founders could come back, have some residual claims, and cause some trouble.

Andre FUD

Andre Conje drove a huge amount of onboarding not only through his popular appeal but also through the projects he built in the Fantom ecosystem. Although he has officially only been an advisor since 2019, he was the primary driving force in writing the aBFT consensus mechanism that underlies Fantom. He built the AMM Solidly, which drove $2+ billion in TVL in just a handful of days. Andre’s sudden departure from crypto drove the value of all of his related projects down. The Fantom Foundation issued a statement clarifying that Cronje was an advisor to the Foundation, but not a core member of the ongoing team. Still, the market has weighed Andre’s departure unfavorably. Since his departure TVL is down 50%, daily new addresses are down over 50%, new coding commitments are close to zero, and transaction volume has dropped.

Centralization

It currently takes 500,000 FTM to run a node. This large monetary commitment threshold has led to the protocol only having 82 validators today. The top five validators control over 800m FTM. To reach the Foundation’s goals of 200–300 validators, the high staking requirement must first be changed via governance. There is an outstanding governance proposal to reduce the minimum stake to 250,000, 100,000, or 50,000 FTM. The vote on this proposal will close in mid-July 2022.

Slashing Risk

If a validator is slashed they face an extremely harsh penalty of 100% of staked tokens being taken away. This penalty extends to those who delegate their stake to a particular validator. In reality, the slashing risk is quite low as it has rarely happened on the network.

Stablecoin Issues

fUSD is a stable coin native to the Fantom ecosystem, that claims to be pegged 1 for 1 to the USD. Since inception, that has clearly not been the case (see below). There have been proposals to try and improve the token structure, but no concrete plans have been finalized.

Source: CoinMarketCap

Validator Rewards Run out in 2023

Today validators are incentivized to secure the network through a combination of the inflation rewards from the initial pre-mine and the 70% cut of transaction fees. Once the pool of rewards expires in 2024, rewards paid to validators will drop dramatically. As a protocol that is popular because of its decentralized finance primitives, the velocity of money is key to power applications, and having no continuous supply of new tokens is a potential issue.

There have been discussions on how to incentivize validators after the existing pool is extinguished. One proposal that has been discussed includes splitting fUSD liquidation fees with validators. Another is to have oracles pay validators to use certain price fees. A third is to implement an EIP-1559-type fee burn mechanism but, instead of burning rewards, to have some percentage go to validators. Although there have been numerous suggestions, there currently is no clear plan on what happens when validator rewards run out.

Conclusion

After a rocky start, Fantom started to gain traction in late 2021 and into 2022, particularly in the DeFi world. The DAG-based blockchain technology is truly a unique approach to solving the blockchain trilemma. Fantom’s valuation metrics are cheap when compared with other major alt-L1s. TVL to market capitalization is the lowest of any major protocol. Revenue also remains higher than popular alternatives like Solana.

But can Fantom sustain its momentum with Andre Cronje gone? TVL in their DeFi primitives has roughly been cut in half since the peak in early March. Much of the strong network and developer usage metrics dropped off after Andre left. It’s also tough to have any near-term conviction on price appreciation for the Fantom token with such a new disconnect between token price and new address growth. The end of inflation rewards in 2024 is a real cause for concern in incentivizing stakers to secure the network.

Fantom is a protocol to keep an eye on as they attempt to regain the momentum they had to start 2022, but at the current juncture, the token remains a risky investment opportunity given so many unknowns.
